abstract: >-
 Interdisciplinary research is a key driver of scientific innovation, yet prior studies primarily examine interdisciplinarity at the whole-paper level, overlooking the functional differences of citations across different sections. It can provide a micro-level understanding of the role of interdisciplinarity in innovation by exploring the relationship between section-based interdisciplinarity and ex-ante novelty as well as ex-post disruption. Using bioinformatics articles, this study firstly calculates section-based interdisciplinarity for the Introduction (I), Methods (M), Results (R), and Discussion (D). Secondly, the ex-ante novelty and ex-post disruption are respectively measured by semantic similarity and the D index. Finally, XGBoost and SHAP model are employed to investigate the relative importance and nonlinear effects of section-based interdisciplinarity. The results show that the XGBoost model outperforms baseline models, with classification performance for ex-ante novelty being significantly stronger than for ex-post disruption. This suggests that section-based interdisciplinarity serves as an effective signal for capturing novelty, whereas disruption, as a lagged indicator, is subject to multiple external factors, limiting model power. SHAP analysis reveals that the section-based interdisciplinarity exerts nonlinear threshold effects. Ex-ante novelty is primarily driven by disparity in the Introduction, but the effect follows an inverted-U pattern where moderate disparity promotes novelty whereas excessive diversity hinders it, indicating that high-quality interdisciplinary integration is more valuable than quantity. By contrast, variety emerges as the most important feature for evaluating ex-post disruption, though its effect declines with increasing variety. The balance and RS(Rao-Stirling) within the Introduction and Results sections show increasing effects, boosting the likelihood of a paper being classified as disruptive, which indicates that disruption stems from the balanced and organic integration of knowledge. Overall, these findings provide a micro-level perspective on how section-specific knowledge integration drives ex-ante novelty and ex-post disruption, offering valuable implications for scientific evaluation and the early detection of innovative contributions.
